PP App

iOS application design

PP is an open source location-based app which helps users to find the closest toilets based on their preferences using their current location.
This app is specifically designed for the Berlin area with its young lifestyle.The vibrant city of Berlin attracts numerous young adults with ambition every year. As a person who enjoys occasional outings in different locations, I always find it difficult to locate a restroom around the area that I’m hanging out with friends.
As I interviewed a few of my colleagues and friends, it was clear to me that this is a common struggle. Not just for nightlife lovers but also for any other type of residence. This common issue motivate me to work on an app that could solve the issue.

Challenges

There were two primary objectives to design the relevant functionality of the app:

  • User demographics
  • Getting access to the list of locations and details

Methods

UX Design methods that were used included:

  • Facilitating workshops
  • Designing questionnaire
  • Interviewing users
  • Designing stories and scenarios
  • Wireframing and prototyping solutions
  • And of course numerous usability testing sessions.

Research

The process started with discovery workshop to have a set of ideas about the topic and  define pain points. After narrowing down ideas with the team, it was time to work on user research to find what we are going to solve and the overall journey of our app.

The goals were

  • How people find bathrooms in Berlin
  • Their experiences using bathrooms in Berlin
  • what are the important toilet facilities for them

The interviews were done in multiple places and times to make sure we interview different types of people with a variety of demographic backgrounds to know who is the main target. The questions were based on findings in a brainstorming session. I narrowed down them to 5-7 top questions and made sure that they were all unique, not leading and open.
After interviews, I gathered and categorized all the information based on the most popular categories using qualitative analysis. The findings helped me to come up with 2 personas.

user persona

To make sure to define the areas that the persona didn’t cover, I worked on the Jobs To Be Done to increase the chance of making a valuable product by asking why the users need a feature instead of focusing on what the problem is or what users want.

Based on the personas and the user interviews, I scripted a typical daily routine for the characters. This process helped us to  decide how the app’s user experience might be designed to fit in with a use case. The stories were defined to

  • Create user flow.
  • Understand the situations that the app is going to be used.
user story

The stories helped us to explore how the product will be used in a broader context, as if it was a portion of a greater narrative. It was an effective and cheaper method to obtain, relate, and investigate the app in real world scenarios.

We used this format to create the stories:

As a (... actor ...), I want to be able to (... function of the system ...), so that (... goal ...) can be achieved.

User Flow & Wireframe

user flow (exhaustive)

To model fluid conversational experiences, I designed the user flow. Defining the user flow helped us to answer two questions:

  • How does the system respond based on a user's behaviour?
  • When a specific bit of information is shown?

Mapping the basic flowchart of the app helped us to figure each step on the path the users will take throughout the solution. The flowchart was initially defined on paper to simplify the test in first steps.

Based on the flowchart the lo-fi wireframe was designed. Applying Crazy Eights method in the first step of wireframing was a huge advantage, it saved us plenty of time and cost. By the end of the section we had multiple design sketches to choose or combine.

Working on the paper wireframing in the first step made iteration quick without feeling attached to the design. After the lo-fi wireframing, I transferred the design to Sketch and prototyped it in InVision to prepare it for the Usability testing phase.

paper wireframe

Usability testing

designing task flows, scenarios and wireframing

As the facilitator, I was responsible to moderate the usability test session. Based on the designed scenarios and task flows, we ran 8 usability test sessions with potential users. The test started with a short introduction and followed up with providing scenarios to the users to go through. During the test I asked a few questions based on the users’ behaviours to understand the thoughts beside the actions. What I had to consider was to not ask leading nor distracting questions and give a chance to the users to express their thoughts on related tasks.

UI

dark-mode design

After refining the wireframes, it was the time to move on to the hi-fi design of the app. The UI started with the Inspiration Board based on effectiveness and functionality of the app. In this step I designed Style Guide, Micro-Interactions, Animation and Onboarding Flow and so on (Light-mode and Dark-mode). In every steps of the design we also considered:

  • Colour blind users
  • Thumb zone heat map
style guide

Conclusion

Working with a passionate team on a non-profitable project was a rewarding journey. From the beginning, I worked alongside a Senior UX designer and a Software Engineer.
In early stages of the project we understood that although the issue was well-known by the users, it is mostly accepted by them as well. Meaning, they found workarounds like using open areas or hidden spots in parks and streets. Therefore, the app should be designed as simple and easy to use as possible so that they can use the app with no effort even when they are under pressure, drunk, at any time of the day (during the day or night). That’s why we also designed the Dark Mode interface. These facts convinced us to put more effort to:

  • Extensive user research
  • Frequently usability test
